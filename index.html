FAQ

Common questions.

Where does the data come from?
PropIntel aggregates from 7 verified sources: Regrid for parcel and ownership records (county assessor data), FEMA National Flood Hazard Layer for flood zone designation, US Census ACS for neighborhood demographics, People Data Labs for skip trace (phones and emails), ATTOM Data for AVM, sold comps, and mortgage history (Pro tier), Geocodio for address normalization, and the FEMA Map Service Center as a verification fallback. All sources are either government databases or licensed commercial aggregators used by institutional lenders and appraisers.
What is the motivation score and how is it calculated?
The motivation score is a 0–100 composite index built from verified ownership signals: Absentee ownership (+25 pts — owner doesn't live at the property), Out-of-state mailing (+20 pts — owner receives mail in a different state), LLC or entity ownership (+10 pts — property held by a company rather than an individual), Long hold duration (+10 pts — owned 10+ years without a sale), and Tax delinquency (+15 pts — owner has fallen behind on property taxes). Scores 0–29 are LOW, 30–59 are MEDIUM, 60+ are HIGH. HIGH-score properties have multiple overlapping signals — these are your best outreach targets. Full breakdown →
What's the difference between assessed value and market value?
Assessed value is the county appraisal district's number — used purely to calculate property taxes. It is not what the property is worth on the open market and is always labeled as "Tax Assessed Value" in your report. Market value is what a buyer and seller would agree to in an arm's-length transaction today. PropIntel shows both — the assessed number from public record and a market range estimate based on comparable sales and AVM data. The gap between them is often meaningful: a large gap (assessed much lower than market) signals significant unrealized equity, which is itself a motivating factor for some sellers. Read more →
How do I read the FEMA flood zone?
The flood zone code determines insurance requirements and risk profile. Zone X = minimal risk, no mandatory flood insurance, standard financing. Zone AE = 100-year floodplain with calculated base flood elevation — flood insurance is required for any federally-backed loan (FHA, VA, conventional). Typically costs $800–$3,000+/year. Zone VE = coastal high-velocity zone, highest risk, most expensive insurance. For any Zone AE property, ask the seller for an elevation certificate — it can significantly reduce insurance premiums if the structure is elevated above BFE. All zone codes explained →
What does the deal analyzer actually calculate?
The deal analyzer runs three acquisition scenarios — at assessed value, market low, and market high — and for each scenario calculates: down payment (25% LTV assumption), loan amount, monthly P&I at current commercial rate, DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio — the ratio of net operating income to annual debt service; lenders typically require 1.25x minimum), and $/SF (price per square foot, for direct comp against market sales). Use the assessed scenario to anchor your offer, market low as your negotiation ceiling, and market high to understand what a full-price buyer pays. Full methodology →
Do you cover all US states?
Base parcel data (owner, assessed value, flood zone, demographics) covers all 50 states. Sold comps and AVM are currently strongest in Texas and major metro markets nationally, with coverage expanding as ATTOM's dataset grows. Skip trace coverage is national. If a Pro report returns limited comp data for a specific market, it will note the data availability — you're never charged for an empty section.
